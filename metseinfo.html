UMGUNGUNDLOVU, A 19TH-CENTURY CAPITAL

These online curation tell stories related to uMgungundlovu, iNkosi Dingane kaSenzangakhona’s capital between 1829 and 1839. Dingane kaSenzangakhona assassinated his brother Shaka in 1828 and took control of the Zulu Kingdom. He built his new capital, uMgungundlovu, in 1829 in the eMakhosini, close to the great places of his ancestors. At the time uMgungundlovu was the largest single settlement that had ever been built in the KwaZulu-Natal region. It was a vibrant centre visited by many people. On 16 December 1838, Voortrekkers led by Andries Pretorius defeated the Zulu forces at iNcome (Blood) River. Dingane retreated northwards after burning uMgungundlovu down. uMgungundlovu existed for just nine years, but the intensity of activity there has left us with abundant remains ranging from artefacts to eye-witness accounts.

A note on sources
In these StoryMaps we use a range of sources that include objects in the collections of the KwaZulu-Natal Museum and other museums, as well as images, descriptions and discussions in published or archival sources. Many materials related to uMgungundlovu and other materials concerning the last five hundred years of southern African history have been digitized by the Five Hundred Year Archive and are available on EMANDULO. One major written source contains conversations by mainly isiZulu-speaking men with an official in the Natal colonial government named James Stuart. Edited and translated transcripts of these conversations have been published in six volumes of the James Stuart Archive (JSA), including most of the first-person descriptions. These quotations are often reproduced verbatim here, and without the benefit of surrounding contextual remarks or the background to the circumstances of any conversations among James Stuart and his various interlocutors. Useful analysis of these issues is provided by Carolyn Hamilton in her 2011 article ‘Backstory, Biography, and the Life of the James Stuart Archive’.
